Blitch couldn't be contacted at Ground Zero, but the institute's president, Louis Clark McCoy, said Blitch's team had taken about a dozen different prototype robots.

"They took everything they could," said McCoy.

"These are military platforms," Blitch told Newsday about the robots at Ground Zero. "They're actually classified systems that we downgraded to bring to this environment."

According to Howie Choset, a roboticist at Carnegie Mellon University who is working on snake-like rescue robots, Blitch's team is using various robots based on Urbie, an extremely tough, semi-autonomous robot developed for military reconnaissance.

"They are really cool remote-controlled cars that provide video and other sensory feedback to the user," Choset said. "It extends the reach of the rescue worker and keeps them out of harm's way."

About the size of a flattened shoe box, Urbie can crawl into tight spaces on its tractor tracks. It can rear up on its tracks to look or crawl over obstacles, and its vision system steers it around objects in its path.

Modified for urban search and rescue, Urbie can be fitted with video and infrared cameras, powerful lights, biological and chemical sensors and voice-activated microphones for communicating with people trapped in the rubble.

Developing robots for search and rescue is an active field in robotics research.

Murphy's group at Florida is one of a handful of research groups worldwide developing search-and-rescue robots. To date, about 40 different rescue robots have been developed.

Not only can robots be sent into spaces too small for humans or dogs, they are dispensable.

As the collapse of the WTC towers tragically demonstrated, rescue work is extremely dangerous. After the Mexico City earthquake, 135 rescuers died during rescue attempts.

Robots are also unaffected by fumes, smoke, fatigue or horror.

Some of the robots at the WTC site appeared at this year's annual Robocup competition, held in Seattle during the International Joint Conference on Artificial Intelligence.

The Robocup competition includes an urban search-and-rescue obstacle course.
